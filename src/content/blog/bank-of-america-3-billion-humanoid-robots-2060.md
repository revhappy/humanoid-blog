Bank of America Global Research has published what may be the most expansive institutional forecast yet on humanoid robotics: 3 billion humanoid robots in service worldwide by 2060, surpassing the roughly 1.5 billion cars currently on the planet. The report, which circulated widely this week, puts hard numbers on a trajectory that the robotics industry has been discussing in more qualitative terms — and the math, if it holds, implies a market transformation that dwarfs most prior technology transitions.

The forecast is not just a headline figure. BofA's analysts lay out a specific growth trajectory, a breakdown by use case, and the structural economic forces they believe will drive adoption. Each deserves examination on its own terms.

## The Growth Trajectory

BofA projects humanoid robot annual shipments will reach approximately 90,000 units in 2026 — a number consistent with announced production targets from Tesla, Figure, Agility, and others. From there, the analysts project growth at an 86% compound annual rate, reaching 1.2 million annual shipments by 2030.

That rate of growth is aggressive by any historical standard, though it is not without precedent in early-stage consumer technology. Smartphone adoption grew at comparable rates in its first decade. The analogy is imperfect — smartphones are far simpler to manufacture and have a much lower unit cost — but the underlying dynamic of rapid price decline driving exponential adoption is structurally similar.

The cost side of the equation is central to BofA's thesis. A Chinese-manufactured humanoid carried a bill-of-materials cost of approximately $35,000 in 2025. BofA projects that falls below $17,000 by 2030. If Chinese manufacturers continue to compress costs at the pace they have in electric vehicles and consumer electronics, $17,000 may prove conservative.

## Where the Robots Will Go

The distribution of 3 billion robots by use case is perhaps the most striking part of the forecast. BofA projects:

- **62% in homes** (approximately 2 billion units)
- **32% in services** — hospitality, healthcare support, retail, logistics
- **3% in industrial applications** — manufacturing, warehousing, construction

The dominance of the home market in BofA's projection is counterintuitive to anyone following the current wave of announcements, which are heavily weighted toward industrial deployments. Tesla Optimus is building toward factory automation. Agility's Digit is in Amazon warehouses. BMW is piloting AEON at Leipzig.

But the analysts' logic is coherent: the home market is ultimately where scale lives. There are roughly 2.1 billion households globally. If even 30% eventually adopt a household robot — a figure that requires a price point well below $10,000 — the resulting unit volumes would dwarf industrial demand by an order of magnitude. BofA's projection implies that the home market unlocks sometime in the 2040s as costs continue to fall and capabilities mature.

## The Structural Drivers

BofA identifies four structural forces making humanoid labor economically attractive over the long run:

**Aging workforces** across developed economies are shrinking the labor supply for physically demanding jobs. Japan, South Korea, Germany, and increasingly China face demographic profiles that guarantee persistent labor shortfalls in caregiving, manufacturing, and logistics.

**Persistent labor shortages** in specific sectors — warehouse workers, home health aides, service industry workers — are structural rather than cyclical. The pandemic accelerated employer willingness to invest in automation as a hedge against labor instability.

**Wage inflation** continues to erode the cost advantage of human labor relative to capital expenditure on automation. At current trajectories, a humanoid robot that costs $17,000 and requires minimal maintenance begins to offer a payback period measured in months for high-utilization industrial applications.

**High employee turnover** in sectors like logistics and food service creates hidden costs — recruiting, training, onboarding — that don't appear in simple wage comparisons. A robot that operates continuously without turnover costs changes the economics significantly.

## Putting the Forecast in Context

BofA's 3 billion figure should be understood as a long-range scenario, not a precise prediction. Forecast horizons of 35 years have enormous uncertainty bands, and the actual outcome will depend on regulatory developments, breakthrough capabilities in robot AI, competitive dynamics in manufacturing, and consumer acceptance patterns that are genuinely difficult to model today.

What the forecast does usefully is establish a credible ceiling scenario for the market and work backward through the enabling conditions required to reach it. For the industry, that exercise clarifies the stakes: if even a fraction of the BofA scenario materializes, humanoid robotics will become one of the defining technology markets of the mid-21st century.

The near-term numbers — 90,000 shipments in 2026, 1.2 million by 2030 — are more actionable benchmarks. Those figures are achievable with announced production plans and will be verifiable within the decade. If that near-term trajectory holds, the longer-range extrapolation becomes considerably more credible.

For investors, policymakers, and the robotics companies themselves, the more important message from BofA may be simpler: the market for humanoid robots is not a niche. It is a platform.

BMW Group has deployed a humanoid robot at its plant in Leipzig, Germany — marking the first time a humanoid has entered a European automotive production environment. The robot, called AEON and developed by Hexagon Robotics, began an initial test deployment in December 2025, with a further phase planned for April 2026 ahead of a full pilot starting in summer.

The move extends a strategy BMW has been building since 2025, when it piloted Figure AI's Figure 02 at its Spartanburg, South Carolina facility — the first humanoid deployment in any BMW factory worldwide.

## Meet AEON: Wheels Instead of Feet

AEON is not a conventional bipedal humanoid. The robot stands 1.65 metres tall and weighs 60 kilograms, but instead of feet, its legs end in wheels. That design choice is deliberate: BMW and Hexagon are prioritizing stability and maneuverability in a production environment over anthropomorphic form. Factory floors present different demands than research labs — they require reliability, repeatability, and the ability to operate safely alongside human workers across long shifts.

The wheeled base gives AEON a lower center of gravity than fully bipedal alternatives, reducing the risk of falls and making it better suited to the structured corridors and workstations of an automotive plant. It is a design philosophy similar to what Sunday Robotics has adopted for its home robot Memo: optimize for the environment, not for humanoid purity.

## From Spartanburg to Leipzig: BMW's Expanding Humanoid Strategy

BMW's humanoid journey began at its Spartanburg plant in South Carolina, where Figure 02 assisted in the production of more than 30,000 BMW X3s over a ten-month pilot in 2025. That deployment provided BMW with real-world data on how humanoid robots perform in production conditions — handling components, navigating shared spaces with human workers, and maintaining throughput.

The Leipzig deployment represents the next phase: scaling the concept to a European facility under different regulatory and operational conditions. BMW has also established a new **Center of Competence for Physical AI in Production** to accelerate the global integration of AI and robotics across its manufacturing network. The center will consolidate learnings from both the Spartanburg and Leipzig pilots and guide future deployments.

## The Role of Hexagon Robotics

Hexagon Robotics is less well-known than Figure or Boston Dynamics, but its selection by BMW signals the company as a serious competitor in the industrial humanoid space. NVIDIA included Hexagon in the partner list announced on March 16 for its physical AI ecosystem — suggesting the company is building on the same foundational AI infrastructure as Figure, AGIBOT, and Agility Robotics.

Hexagon's parent company, Hexagon AB, is a global leader in measurement and manufacturing software — a background that gives the robotics division deep expertise in precision manufacturing environments. That heritage likely informed the design of AEON: a robot optimized for the dimensional precision and process consistency that automotive production demands.

## Timeline and Scope

BMW's deployment roadmap at Leipzig follows a staged approach:

1. **December 2025**: Initial test deployment at Plant Leipzig — evaluation and laboratory integration
2. **April 2026**: Second test deployment phase — operational integration and worker co-habituation
3. **Summer 2026**: Full pilot phase begins — production-integrated operation at scale

BMW has not disclosed what specific tasks AEON will perform during the pilot, though the company has indicated the focus is on tasks that complement human workers rather than replace them — consistent with the "collaborative automation" framing it used during the Spartanburg pilot.

## What the Leipzig Deployment Signals

The European automotive sector has been slower to adopt humanoid robotics than its US and Chinese counterparts, partly due to stronger labor regulations and partly due to the traditionally conservative pace of change in established manufacturing facilities. BMW's decision to push ahead at Leipzig — under Germany's labor framework and with the attention of European regulators — is a meaningful signal for the broader industry.

If the Leipzig pilot succeeds on the same terms as Spartanburg, it will demonstrate that humanoid robot deployments in production are repeatable across geographies and regulatory environments. That reproducibility is what transforms a proof of concept into an industry standard.

The automotive sector moves slowly, but it moves at scale. BMW produces roughly 2.5 million vehicles per year across its global network. A humanoid deployment approach that works in both South Carolina and Saxony has the potential to propagate across dozens of plants — and the entire automotive sector will be watching closely.

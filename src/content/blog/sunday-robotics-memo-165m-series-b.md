Sunday Robotics announced on March 12 that it has raised an oversubscribed $165 million Series B at a $1.15 billion valuation — vaulting the household robotics startup into unicorn territory and setting the stage for its first consumer deployments this year. The round was led by Coatue Management, with participation from Bain Capital Ventures, Fidelity Management & Research, Tiger Global, Benchmark, Conviction, and Xtal Ventures.

The company's robot, called **Memo**, is designed to handle routine household tasks — clearing tables, loading dishwashers, folding laundry, operating espresso machines — and Sunday says it plans to put Memo into beta users' homes before Thanksgiving 2026.

## Memo: A Different Kind of Home Robot

Memo is not a bipedal humanoid in the traditional sense. Founded by Tony Zhao and Cheng Chi, Sunday has deliberately chosen a wheeled, low-center-of-gravity form factor over a walking design. The rationale is practical: homes are complex, cluttered, and populated by children and pets. A robot that can tip over or stumble poses risks that consumer customers are unlikely to tolerate.

The wheeled base allows Memo to navigate residential spaces reliably while its upper body handles manipulation tasks. The design echoes a pattern emerging across multiple segments of the industry — both BMW's AEON factory robot and Sunday's Memo prioritize stability over humanoid form, optimizing for environment fit rather than anthropomorphic aesthetics.

What sets Memo apart is the data foundation behind its capabilities. Sunday has collected approximately **10 million real-world household episodes** from more than 500 homes using a proprietary **Skill Capture Glove** system — a device human demonstrators wear to teach the robot new tasks. That dataset scale is unusual for a startup and reflects a deliberate strategy of grounding robot behavior in genuine household variability rather than controlled lab conditions.

## The Home Robotics Race Heats Up

Sunday's raise arrives at a moment when the case for household robotics is gaining serious institutional backing. Bank of America's recently published forecast projects that 62% of all humanoid robots in service by 2060 — approximately 2 billion units — will be deployed in homes. The BofA analysts identified aging populations, persistent labor shortages, and declining unit costs as the structural drivers.

Sunday is betting it can be the product that bridges the gap between today's industrial robot deployments and that longer-range consumer scenario. The company's focus on multi-step household tasks — not just a single gimmick like vacuuming — reflects an understanding that consumer robots need to deliver real utility before they earn a place in the home.

The funding round's investor roster is notable for its quality as much as its size. Coatue, Tiger Global, and Benchmark are not robotics specialists — they are top-tier crossover funds that invest in companies they believe have the potential to become defining platforms. Their presence in Sunday's Series B signals that at least some sophisticated investors believe the home robot market is approaching an inflection point.

## Road to Launch

Sunday's stated goal is to deploy autonomous robots to consumer homes by the end of 2026. The company has framed the upcoming beta phase as a critical learning opportunity — a chance to understand real-world user needs and refine Memo before scaling to thousands of households.

The timeline is ambitious. Taking a household robot from lab-validated capability to reliable, safe, consumer-scale deployment involves challenges that laboratory testing cannot fully anticipate: the infinite variability of real home layouts, the presence of pets and children, user expectations calibrated by decades of fictional robots, and the safety standards required for a product operating autonomously in a living space.

Sunday's 10-million-episode training dataset — gathered from real homes rather than simulated environments — is its primary hedge against those challenges. The Skill Capture Glove approach is also a sustainable data collection mechanism: as the beta fleet grows, the company can continue gathering household training data at scale, creating a compounding capability advantage over time.

## What Comes Next

The Series B funds will go directly toward the beta deployment and the engineering work required to scale from hundreds to thousands of units. Coatue's Thomas Laffont joins Sunday's board as part of the deal — bringing institutional governance experience that will matter as the company transitions from R&D startup to consumer product company.

Sunday's Thanksgiving 2026 target puts it in direct competition with other home robot efforts expected to materialize over the same period. The race for the living room is accelerating, and Sunday has just secured the runway to be a serious contender when it arrives.

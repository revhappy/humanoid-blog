Figure AI has unveiled Figure 03, its third-generation humanoid robot, alongside Helix 02 — the most advanced version of its robot AI to date. Together, they represent a step change in what Figure is building: Figure 03 is the company's first robot designed from the ground up for high-volume manufacturing, and Helix 02 is an AI system that treats the entire robot body as a single coordinated machine rather than a collection of separately controlled parts.

The combined announcement positions Figure at the frontier of the growing competition to build a humanoid robot capable of operating reliably in homes and workplaces without human supervision.

## Figure 03: Built for Scale

Every previous Figure robot was, to some degree, a research and demonstration platform. Figure 03 is different. The company explicitly describes it as "engineered from the ground up for high-volume manufacturing" — a design constraint that touches everything from material choices to assembly sequences to component standardization.

The hardware reflects this shift in several notable ways:

- **Next-generation vision system**: Delivers twice the frame rate, one-quarter the latency, and a 60% wider field of view per camera compared to Figure 02. For a robot that navigates real-world environments and handles objects, these are not incremental improvements — they directly affect what the robot can perceive and how quickly it can respond.
- **Wireless inductive charging**: Charging coils are embedded in the robot's feet, allowing it to step onto a wireless charging stand and recharge at 2 kW without any physical connection. This design choice eliminates a significant failure point in long-duration deployments — cable connections that wear out or are accidentally disconnected.
- **Manufacturing-first design**: Figure has not disclosed full specifications, but the emphasis on manufacturability suggests the company is preparing for production volumes that would make component sourcing and assembly reliability critical constraints.

The target price point for Figure 03 is approximately $20,000 for consumer purchase — a significant reduction from earlier generation costs and a figure that, if achievable, would put Figure in reach of a much broader market than enterprise-only customers.

## Helix 02: One Brain for the Whole Body

Helix 02 is the more technically consequential part of the announcement. Previous robot AI systems typically separate locomotion from manipulation — one system handles walking and balance, another handles arm and hand movements. The coordination between these systems creates latency, inconsistency, and brittle failure modes when the robot encounters unexpected situations.

Helix 02 abandons that architecture entirely. It connects every onboard sensor — vision, touch, and proprioception — directly to every actuator through a single unified visuomotor neural network. The result is what Figure calls **continuous, room-scale autonomy**: the robot can walk, handle objects, balance, and adapt simultaneously without human input, because all of those functions are being computed as one integrated system.

The sensing capabilities that feed into Helix 02 are similarly unified:

- **Palm cameras** provide in-hand visual feedback when objects are occluded from the head camera — a critical capability for tasks like picking up objects from inside a bag or retrieving items from a shelf
- **Tactile sensors** in each fingertip detect forces as small as three grams, enabling the fine grip modulation required for delicate objects
- **Proprioceptive feedback** from joints and actuators feeds into the same network, allowing the robot to feel its way through tasks that vision alone cannot resolve

The practical implication of Helix 02 is that Figure 03 can walk into a room, locate an object, pick it up, carry it while continuing to navigate, and set it down — all as a continuous action rather than a sequence of discrete steps handed off between separate subsystems.

## BotQ: The Manufacturing Infrastructure

Scaling a robot like Figure 03 to consumer volumes requires manufacturing infrastructure that most robotics companies have never built. Figure has established **BotQ**, a dedicated manufacturing facility designed specifically for humanoid robot production.

BotQ's first-generation line is capable of producing up to 12,000 humanoid robots per year, with a stated goal of reaching 100,000 units over the next four years. That trajectory — from 12,000 to 100,000 — implies the company is planning multiple generations of production line expansion in parallel with Figure 03's commercial rollout.

The manufacturing challenge is distinct from the AI and hardware challenges. Consistent quality at volume, supply chain reliability for specialized components, and the logistics of shipping and servicing complex physical machines are problems that software companies-turned-hardware companies consistently underestimate. BotQ's existence as a standalone facility, rather than a contracted manufacturing partner, suggests Figure has internalized this lesson.

## What This Means for the Market

Figure 03 and Helix 02 raise the bar for what the industry considers a production-ready humanoid. The combination of high-frequency perception, full-body unified control, and wireless charging creates a robot that is meaningfully more capable and more deployable than Figure 02.

The $20,000 target price, if achieved, also changes the competitive calculus. At that price point, Figure enters the range where early adopter consumers — not just enterprise buyers — could consider purchase. Combined with the Helix deployment timeline targeting broader home availability by late 2026, Figure is making a direct play for the consumer market that BofA's analysts project will ultimately dwarf industrial demand.

The race to the living room is now formally underway. Figure has the hardware, the AI, and the manufacturing infrastructure to compete. The question, as always with robotics, is execution.
